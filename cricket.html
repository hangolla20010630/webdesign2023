CRICKET

Featuring two teams of eleven players each, cricket is a team sport. A flat, 20.12 m (22 yards) long strip of land known as a pitch sits in the middle of the roughly oval grass field where the bat and ball game is played. A wicket is a group of wooden stumps that are located at each end of the playing area. Be aware that the pitch itself is frequently used as the term "wicket," which can be somewhat misleading. A firm, fist-sized leather ball with a cork centre is thrown from one wicket to the other by a member of the fielding team. Before the batsman from the opposite team, who uses a wooden cricket bat to protect the wicket from the ball, the ball typically bounces once before reaching him. A second batsman (the "non-striker") who has been standing inactively close to the bowler's wicket can then exchange ends with the batsman by running between the wickets in order to score runs. The remaining members of the bowlers' squad are fielders who are positioned in various spots all around the oval. For many years, cricket has been a recognised team sport.

The majority of its followers are current and past Commonwealth members, where it got its modern form's start. Cricket is by far the most well-liked sport in certain South Asian nations, including India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Sri Lanka. In addition to these nations, cricket is a popular sport in England and Wales, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, Zimbabwe, and the English-speaking Caribbean nations collectively known as the West Indies. The Netherlands, Kenya, Nepal, and Argentina are just a few examples of the diverse nations that host well-established amateur club events.

HISTORY OF THE SPORT

Cricket is thought to have started out as a kid's game in the southeast counties of England. The word "cricket" for the sport is derived from the Old English word "cryce," which denotes a stick. Because the scorer used to notch tally sticks to count the amount of runs at the time, runs were referred to as "notches" during that era. The sport had a low-key following for a considerable amount of time before it began to spread outside of England at the turn of the century.

Cricket was adopted as England's national sport in the 18th century, and the 52nd English cricket season, which began in 1748, is the oldest season of the game that is known to have been played. This popularity allowed cricket to finally find a home in the hearts of its fans. The first international tour of English players took place in 1859, and the two decades leading up to World War I are referred to as the "Golden Age of Cricket" since they saw a number of notable matches and the development of many outstanding players. The first-ever Test match between England and Australia was contested at the MCC Ground in 1876â€“1877, and it was the moment the rivalry gave rise to The Ashes Series in 1882.

During the 20th century, the popularity of Test cricket grew throughout the world, and countries including Bangladesh, West Indies, New Zealand, India, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, and Zimbabwe began to play this format. A new version of cricket limited overs was launched in 1963 by English counties.

In 1971, the first limited-overs international tournament was held, and in 1975, the first such World Cup. The sport immediately benefited from the introduction of a new limited-overs format in the twenty-first century, which drew large crowds of spectators and participants.

RULES AND REGULATIONS

OBJECTIVE

The fundamental objective of the game is to outscore your opponent by scoring more runs by hitting fours, sixes, and sprinting between the wickets to win the game.

PLAYING AREA

Although a cricket pitch is typically slightly oval, a circular field is thought to be the ideal one. Its diameter ranges from 137 to 150 metres. The playing area is specified as a minimum of 137.16m from boundary to boundary square of the pitch under the ICC Test Match Standard Playing Conditions (October 2014) Law 19.1, with the shorter of the two square boundaries being a minimum of 59.43m. The pitch's straight boundary is at least 64 metres wide at both ends. Distances are gauged from the pitch's centre Boundaries cannot extend farther than 82.29 metres from the pitch's centre.

STARTING PLAY

  • Test match-: 10 a.m. to 5.30 p.m.

  • ODI match-: 2.30 a.m. to 11.30 p.m.

  • T20I match-: 7 p.m. to 11.00 p.m.

SCORING

A striker must hit the ball and sprint to the other end of the pitch while his non-striking partner sprints to his end in order to score a run. Both runners must make contact with the ground behind the popping crease with either their bats or their bodies for a run to be recorded. The batsman may double back to score two or more runs if the striker smashes the ball sufficiently enough. Running between wickets is the term for this. There is no regulation requiring the batsman to run after striking the ball, though. If the batsmen score an odd number of runs, they will have switched ends and, unless the most recent ball signals the end of an over, their roles as striker and non-striker will be reversed for the next ball. The closest batter is run out if a fielder uses the ball to knock the bails off the stumps while no one is grounded behind the closest popping crease. Four runs are scored if the ball crosses the boundary, or six if it doesn't bounce.

FUNDAMENTALS

The fundamental skills involved in the game, include:

  • Hand-Eye Coordination

  • Concentration

  • Refelexes

  • Spatial Perception

  • Sprinting and Agility

  • Timing

  • Balance

  • Strength and Flexibility

GAME DURATION

In the Test cricket match 7 hours and 30 minutes each day with 40 minutes lunch break and 20 minutes tea break. In the ODI cricket match 3 hours and 30 minutes each inning with 45 minutes innings break. In the T20I cricket match 1 hour and 20 minutes each inning with 10 minutes innings break.
